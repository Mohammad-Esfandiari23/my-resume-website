My Story

The Beginning: A Passion for the Brain

Ever since I can remember, I have been fascinated by the complexity of the brain—how it functions, how it shapes our perception, and how it enables us to interact with the world. As a teenager, I knew I wanted to study psychology to explore these mysteries. However, in Iran, pursuing psychology at the university level required choosing the humanities track in high school— a path that heavily focused on abstract concepts rather than the tangible, hands-on problem-solving that I enjoyed.

This choice surprised those around me because, despite my passion for psychology, I had always excelled in building, experimenting, and applied sciences. During my high school years, I won third place nationwide in the Kharazmi Festival, a prestigious scientific competition, for designing a unique electrical device with innovative features. My ability to combine technical knowledge with problem-solving became clear at an early stage.

Determined to pursue psychology despite these expectations, I prepared for Iran’s highly competitive National University Entrance Exam (Konkur). Out of 282,151 applicants, I ranked in the top 0.2093%, securing a place at Shahid Beheshti University, one of Iran’s top three psychology programs. I majored in Counseling Psychology, a field focused on the practical applications of psychological theories, particularly in therapy and mental health interventions.

A Turning Point: The Need for Experimental Science

By the end of my third year of undergraduate studies, I felt increasingly dissatisfied. While I appreciated the theoretical foundations of psychology, I craved something more tangible, empirical, and measurable. I wanted to go beyond reading established theories and start testing hypotheses, running experiments, and working with real data.

This realization led me toward data analysis and statistics. I became proficient in SPSS and began collaborating with students on research projects by handling their statistical analyses. One of these projects examined how maladaptive perfectionism contributes to heightened social anxiety, ultimately hindering athletic performance. This experience helped me develop my skills in statistical modeling and hypothesis testing, reinforcing my desire to transition into a more experimental, quantitative approach to psychology.

My graduation ceremony from my undergraduate program.

Discovering Neuroscience

My growing interest in data-driven research naturally led me to the field of neuroscience. I became captivated by the idea of understanding cognition through neural mechanisms, computational approaches, and behavioral data analysis. Since Iran does not offer a dedicated neuroscience master’s program, I chose to pursue my Master’s in General Psychology— a field that, in Iran, heavily emphasizes experimental psychology and shares significant overlap with cognitive neuroscience.

To secure admission into this program, I took the highly competitive Iranian National Graduate Entrance Exam, ranking in the top 0.09% of 80,000 applicants. This achievement allowed me to join Allameh Tabataba’i University, one of Iran’s top three psychology departments.

During my graduate studies, I focused on refining my programming skills, incorporating Python and MATLAB into my research. This skill set allowed me to work on more advanced experimental paradigms, leading me to my current focus: reaction time modeling and temporal prediction.

My current research investigates how reaction times vary across sensory modalities (e.g., vision and motor responses) and how the brain encodes time to predict future events. I am currently working on a research paper in this domain, which is expected to be published within the next six months.
